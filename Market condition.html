How to read these indicators

Each card shows one indicator's current reading and history. The flag in the top-right of each card summarises stress level using thresholds calibrated on long-term history:

NORMAL reading is in a healthy range ELEVATED reading is approaching extreme territory; watch closely EXTREME reading is at historically dangerous levels

Important: these are diagnostic indicators, not timing signals on their own. Conditions can stay extreme for months or years before resolving. The strategy combines these with price action (Section II) for actual trade decisions.

Excess Leverage Growth — deep dive —

Definition: Margin Debt year-over-year growth minus SPX year-over-year growth. Measures whether investors are actively borrowing more on top of price gains (positive) or letting collateral appreciation outpace new borrowing (negative).

How to read it:

  • Positive (red) — active speculative leveraging: people are taking on new debt faster than prices are rising. Historically rare; preceded both the 2000 dot-com peak and the 2007 GFC.
  • Negative (green) — healthy bull or active deleveraging: prices rising faster than borrowing, or unwinding of past leverage. The 2010s QE era was mostly green.
  • Above +20% — warning threshold: historically only crossed at major bull market peaks.

Why this matters: the standard Margin/Currency ratio can stay extreme for years simply because rising collateral mechanically expands borrowing capacity (passive). Excess Leverage Growth filters out passive expansion and isolates moments when borrowing is genuinely speculative.
